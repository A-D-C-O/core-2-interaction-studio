What is interactivity?

We typically think of interactivity in the context of computing and the web as a two-directional process:


I visit a site → it prompts me with a set of possible actions or options → I respond → the window responds in turn (it moves; content is displayed or retracted; new options are presented; etc.) → we repeat the process based on the results

What if we roll this definition back to its basic elements?

What if rather than thinking in the terms of UI/UX (things like, “clickstream analysis,” “A/B testing,” or “design thinking”), in which specified behaviors are elicited from visitors, we instead thought of interaction as beginning with a shift in someone’s attention? What if rather than serving other content (i.e. news, memes, a social network) a website asked us to spend some time focusing itself, its behaviors and features, its own presence?

What is “ambient”?

The dictionary defines “ambient” as the things that make up our surroundings or environment. Often these aren’t what we focus on. Instead, they’re usually the grounds against which other things happen. Take our classroom for example: we focus on the light being thrown from the projector against the screen, but there are other sources of light in the room. We don’t attend to the color of the screen, but it influences everything we see. These are features that make up our ambient environment.

What is an “ambient website”?

Let spend some time building websites that shift our attention. The interaction experience that these should offer is the opportunity to observe. As such, let’s have them change over time as our environments do throughout the day. These changes might be fast or slow, loud or subtle.

With HTML and CSS, let’s make use of the @keframes rule that we looked at last class to modulate the elements on our page in ways that a visitor might want to observe.

A reminder of @keyframes syntax:
